Will Conversation Guide
These questions are designed to be asked in conversation — not answered on a form. Record the session in Otter, then paste the AI summary into the Summary Notes tab. The goal is to capture intent, not just facts — so the family understands the decisions when it matters most.
1
Can you walk me through how you've decided to divide your estate — and what was behind those decisions?
This opens the conversation broadly and lets the client lead. You're listening for the reasoning — who they trust, who they feel responsible for, and what fairness means to them.
2
Are there any decisions in your will that might surprise the people you love — and is that intentional?
This surfaces uncomfortable truths gently. If someone is treated differently — less, or left out entirely — there's usually a reason. The question lets the client name it without being asked directly.
3
What do you want your family to understand about why you made the choices you made?
This is the legacy message question. Some clients want their children to know "this was about fairness, not favouritism." This is where a letter of wishes or ethical will might come from.
4
Are there any family dynamics — relationships, situations, or histories — that could affect how your estate is received?
Blended families, estrangements, family members with financial difficulties — this question allows the client to flag complexity without being asked to name specific conflicts.
5
Is there anything you own that has significant sentimental value — something that matters more for what it represents than what it's worth?
A lake property, a piece of jewellery, a family heirloom. These often create the most conflict because the will treats them like financial assets when they carry emotional weight.
6
Does your executor know they've been named — and have you talked with them about what's actually involved?
Many people are named as executor without being told. The job can take months or years. This question often reveals the conversation hasn't happened yet.
7
How do you feel about leaving a large lump sum to your beneficiaries — is there anything about timing or conditions you've thought about?
For clients with young adult children or a beneficiary who struggles with money — a testamentary trust or staggered distributions may be worth exploring.
8
Is there a cause, a community, or an institution you want to remember in your will?
Charitable bequests are often something clients want but haven't been asked about. A percentage of residue or a named gift to a hospital or foundation — this question surfaces intention that might otherwise go unrecorded.
9
If both of you were gone at the same time — what would you want the world to look like for the people you're leaving behind?
This is the hardest question and the most important. It gets at guardianship, the kids' education, the family home, who holds things together. The conversation often reveals the will doesn't yet reflect these wishes clearly.
10
When this is all said and done — what do you want your legacy to feel like to the people you love most?
This is the closing question. Not what they own — who they are and what they stood for. Some clients have strong feelings here. That's the right answer. Record it carefully.

Power of Attorney Conversation Guide
The POA conversation is about trust, values, and preparation. You're capturing why they chose their attorney and whether that person is actually ready for the responsibility. Record in Otter, summarize with AI, paste into Summary Notes.
1
If you were in an accident tomorrow and couldn't make financial or medical decisions — who do you trust most to step in?
This is deliberately direct. It removes the legal language and gets straight to the human reality. Listen for who they name instinctively — and whether that matches who's currently on the document.
2
Why did you choose this person specifically? What is it about them that makes you confident they'd make good decisions on your behalf?
This surfaces the qualities the client values — judgment, trustworthiness, level-headedness, financial familiarity. It can also reveal the choice was made out of obligation rather than genuine confidence.
3
Does your attorney know they've been named — and have you actually had the conversation with them about what you'd want?
Many people are named as attorney without being told. The POA document alone isn't enough — the attorney needs to know the client's wishes, values, and expectations. This question often reveals the conversation is overdue.
4
When it comes to managing your finances, what would you want your attorney to know about how you think about money?
Values around spending, saving, risk, family support, charitable giving — these don't appear in the legal document but matter enormously when someone manages your affairs.
5
Are there any financial decisions you'd want your attorney to consult others about before acting?
Some clients want a second opinion required for large gifts, property sales, or investment decisions. Others want full authority given. This surfaces conditions or limitations that should be built into the document.
6
If you needed to move to a care facility, what would you want your attorney to know about your wishes for your quality of life?
Level of care, where they'd want to live, maintaining dignity and independence, staying close to family — these personal care decisions go beyond the legal document. A well-prepared attorney needs to know these things.
7
Is there anyone in your life you specifically would NOT want involved in your financial or personal affairs — and why?
Family conflict, estrangements, a person who might unduly influence decisions — sometimes the most important thing a POA does is exclude certain people. The client needs to be able to name this.
8
What does maintaining your independence look like to you — and how long would you want to fight for it?
Some clients want every measure taken to remain independent. Others would rather transition to supported care earlier and focus on quality of life. This shapes how the attorney approaches decisions about living arrangements and resources.
9
Are you aware that your POA can be activated while you're still alive and capable, if you choose — not just when incapacity occurs?
Many clients don't know this. A continuing POA can be used proactively — during travel, illness, or for convenience. This often opens a practical conversation about whether they want to activate it sooner than planned.
10
What's the one thing you'd most want the person holding your POA to remember when they're making decisions for you?
This closing question often produces the most important answer. It's direct, personal, and gets to the heart of what the client trusts this person to honour. Record it carefully.

Health Directive Conversation Guide
This is the most personal conversation of the three — and the most important. You're giving someone the chance to be heard about what matters most when they can no longer speak for themselves. Go slowly. Let silence sit. Record in Otter, summarize with AI, paste into Summary Notes.
1
Have you ever watched someone go through a serious health crisis or end-of-life situation — and did it change how you think about your own wishes?
This opens through lived experience rather than abstract planning. It's much easier to talk about what you witnessed and felt than to answer hypothetical questions cold. This often becomes the frame for everything that follows.
2
What does quality of life mean to you — what would have to be true for life to still feel worth living?
This is the foundational question for any health directive. The answer is completely personal — for one person it's recognising their family, for another it's physical independence. There's no right answer, but there's usually a clear one.
3
If you were seriously ill with no reasonable chance of recovery — what would you want your medical care to focus on?
Comfort, pain management, time to say goodbye, being at home — this question gets at the end-of-life priorities that the health directive should reflect.
4
How do you feel about life support and resuscitation — under what circumstances would you want everything done, and when would you want it stopped?
The DNR question — framed with compassion. Many people want "everything done if there's a real chance" but not "everything done to extend dying." That distinction matters enormously and needs to be on the record.
5
Who in your life do you trust most to make medical decisions for you when you cannot — and what makes you confident in that choice?
The SDM is often chosen by default rather than design. This question helps the client think through whether the right person is named — and whether that person knows what they'd want.
6
Have you talked to your substitute decision maker about your wishes — do they actually know what you'd want?
Being named as SDM without context is one of the hardest positions to be in. This question often reveals the conversation is overdue — and becomes an action item out of the meeting.
7
Where would you want to be cared for — and where would you want to be when you die, if you had a choice?
Home, a hospice, near family — these deeply personal wishes rarely make it into legal documents. Capturing this creates a roadmap for the SDM that goes beyond any form.
8
Are there any cultural, spiritual, or religious wishes that should shape the decisions made on your behalf?
Faith traditions, rituals, prayers, cultural practices around death and dying — these matter deeply to some clients and are invisible to the healthcare system unless they're on record.
9
Is there a message you want to leave for the people you love — something you'd want them to know or remember?
This question surfaces what the client most wants honoured at the end of their life. Some clients write letters. Some record a video. Some just want the answer on file with someone who cares. Record it as they give it.
10
What would a good death look like to you — and what would feel like a hard one?
This is the most direct question and also the most important. It removes euphemism and gets to the heart of what the health directive is for. Many clients have never been asked this. Give them time. The answer is the most important thing you'll write down today.
